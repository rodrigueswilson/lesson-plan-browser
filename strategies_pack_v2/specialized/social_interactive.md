### Strategy ID: collaborative_learning

**Name:** Collaborative Learning with L1 Support

**Aliases:** Cooperative Learning, Think-Pair-Share

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Social & Interactive Learning

**Primary Skill:** speaking

**Secondary Skills:** listening, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.5; listening: 0.1667; reading: 0.1667; writing: 0.1667

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, small-group, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Use structured peer interaction, allowing L1 discussion toward English output.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Group problem-solving
- Peer tutoring
- Think–pair–share

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Group same-L1 peers for comprehension in L1; require English output products.
2. Provide protocols and sentence frames; monitor and debrief.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Increases interaction and confidence
- Improves quality of English output

**Research Foundation:** Margarita Calderón, Robert Slavin, Spencer Kagan

**Cross-References:** translanguaging, siop

**Short Definition:**
Structured peer interaction that promotes negotiation of meaning, language practice, and co-construction of knowledge.

**Long Definition:**
Collaborative Learning involves structured peer activities in which students jointly engage in tasks that require both academic thinking and language use. Typical formats include think‑pair‑share, jigsaw reading, expert groups, and project-based group work. Teachers set clear roles, provide sentence frames, and monitor to ensure equitable participation. Collaboration prompts students to negotiate meaning, clarify concepts, and use academic language functions such as justifying, comparing, or predicting. It supports both comprehension (listening to peers) and production (speaking/writing to contribute). Because peers often share linguistic resources, bilingual explanations can surface naturally and can be bridged to English output. Collaborative learning is powerful for engagement and oral language development but requires explicit norms; without structure, some students may dominate while others remain silent. Teachers must circulate, scaffold language, and hold groups accountable for both process and product.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Clear roles and structured protocols for group interaction
- Sentence frames and language scaffolds for academic discussion
- L1 discussion opportunities bridging to English output
- Equitable participation with teacher monitoring and support

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Unstructured group work without clear roles or language support
- Dominant students doing all the talking without participation protocols
- Group work without accountability for both process and academic product

### Strategy ID: think_pair_share

**Name:** Think-Pair-Share

**Aliases:** TPS, Structured discussion

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Social & Interactive Learning

**Primary Skill:** speaking

**Secondary Skills:** listening

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.7; listening: 0.3; reading: 0; writing: 0

**Delivery Mode:** whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Structure individual thinking, paired discussion, and whole-group sharing to maximize participation.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Discussion activities
- Comprehension checks
- Problem-solving tasks

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Pose question and allow individual think time.
2. Pair students for L1 discussion if beneficial.
3. Share responses with whole group in English.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Increases student participation
- Builds confidence before public speaking

**Research Foundation:** Frank Lyman, Spencer Kagan

**Cross-References:** collaborative_learning, sentence_frames

**Short Definition:**
Three-phase discussion structure: individual thinking, paired discussion, and whole-group sharing.

**Long Definition:**
Think-Pair-Share is a structured discussion strategy that maximizes student participation through three distinct phases: individual thinking time, paired discussion, and whole-group sharing. Teachers pose a question or problem and provide wait time for individual reflection, allowing all students to formulate initial thoughts. Students then pair with a partner to discuss their ideas, which may include L1 discussion for comprehension and confidence-building. Finally, pairs share their thinking with the whole group, typically in English. This structure ensures that all students have opportunities to process information, practice language, and contribute to class discussions. For multilingual learners, the paired discussion phase is particularly valuable as it provides a low-stakes environment to rehearse ideas and language before public sharing. The strategy builds confidence, increases participation, and supports both content learning and language development.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Clear three-phase structure with adequate time for each phase
- Individual think time before paired discussion
- Strategic pairing that may include L1 discussion opportunities
- Whole-group sharing that builds on paired conversations

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Immediate whole-group discussion without think or pair time
- Rushed phases without adequate processing time
- Pairing without consideration of language support needs
