### Strategy ID: formative_assessment_ml

**Name:** Formative Assessment for Multilingual Learners

**Aliases:** Assessment for learning, Ongoing assessment

**Present In:** Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** listening, speaking, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Gather evidence of learning to adjust instruction and provide actionable feedback in real time.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Daily instruction across subjects
- Assess content understanding and language development

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Co-define goals/success criteria; collect varied evidence.
2. Give timely feedback; allow L1 to assess content knowledge accurately.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Closes learning gaps quickly
- Promotes agency and growth mindset

**Research Foundation:** Paul Black, Dylan Wiliam, Margaret Heritage

**Cross-References:** differentiated_instruction, scaffolded_instruction

**Short Definition:**
Ongoing assessment practices that gather evidence of learning to adjust instruction and provide feedback for multilingual learners.

**Long Definition:**
Formative Assessment for Multilingual Learners involves systematic collection of evidence about student learning during instruction to make real-time adjustments and provide actionable feedback. For multilingual learners, this includes separating content knowledge from language proficiency, using multiple modalities for assessment, and allowing L1 use when appropriate to accurately gauge understanding. Teachers use various techniques such as exit tickets, thumbs up/down, peer discussions, and quick writes to monitor both content comprehension and language development. The approach emphasizes co-constructed success criteria, timely feedback, and student self-assessment opportunities. Effective formative assessment for multilingual learners recognizes that language development takes time and provides multiple ways for students to demonstrate their knowledge while building English proficiency.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Multiple assessment modalities accommodating language proficiency levels
- Separation of content knowledge from English language proficiency
- Real-time instructional adjustments based on assessment evidence
- Student self-assessment and goal-setting opportunities

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Only paper-and-pencil assessments requiring high English proficiency
- Assessment without follow-up instructional adjustments
- Confusing content knowledge with English language ability

### Strategy ID: portfolio_assessment

**Name:** Portfolio Assessment

**Aliases:** Learning portfolios, Growth documentation

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** writing, speaking, reading

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.2; listening: 0.1; reading: 0.3; writing: 0.4

**Delivery Mode:** individual, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Collect student work over time to document growth and provide authentic assessment opportunities.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Long-term projects
- Language development tracking
- Student reflection activities

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Establish clear portfolio criteria and organization system.
2. Include student reflections and goal-setting in both L1 and English.
3. Use portfolios for student-led conferences and growth celebrations.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Documents growth over time
- Promotes student ownership and reflection

**Research Foundation:** Roberta Tierney, Mark Simon, Paulette Bondy

**Cross-References:** formative_assessment_ml, student_self_assessment

**Short Definition:**
Systematic collection of student work over time to document learning growth and provide authentic assessment.

**Long Definition:**
Portfolio Assessment involves systematic collection and organization of student work over time to document learning growth, provide authentic assessment opportunities, and promote student reflection. For multilingual learners, portfolios can include work in both L1 and English, showing language development progression and content learning across languages. Students actively participate in selecting work, writing reflections, and setting goals for future learning. Portfolios may include writing samples, audio recordings, project documentation, self-assessments, and peer feedback. This approach allows teachers, students, and families to see growth over time rather than focusing on single-point assessments. Effective portfolio implementation includes clear criteria, regular conferences, and opportunities for students to share their learning journey with authentic audiences.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Systematic collection and organization of work over time
- Student participation in work selection and reflection processes
- Documentation of growth in both content and language development
- Regular portfolio conferences and goal-setting sessions

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Random collection of work without clear purpose or criteria
- Teacher-only selection without student input or reflection
- Portfolios that sit unused without regular review or conferences

### Strategy ID: peer_assessment_bilingual

**Name:** Peer Assessment in Bilingual Settings

**Aliases:** Collaborative assessment, Peer feedback

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Assessment & Scaffolding, Social & Interactive Learning

**Primary Skill:** speaking

**Secondary Skills:** listening, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.5; listening: 0.3; reading: 0.1; writing: 0.1

**Delivery Mode:** small-group, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Engage students in providing structured feedback to peers using both L1 and English as appropriate.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Writing workshops
- Project presentations
- Collaborative learning activities

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Teach specific feedback protocols and sentence frames.
2. Allow peer discussions in L1 before providing English feedback.
3. Model respectful, constructive feedback practices.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Develops critical thinking and communication skills
- Provides multiple perspectives on learning

**Research Foundation:** Keith Topping, Nancy Falchikov

**Cross-References:** collaborative_learning, sentence_frames

**Short Definition:**
Structured process where students provide feedback to peers using appropriate language supports and protocols.

**Long Definition:**
Peer Assessment in Bilingual Settings involves teaching students to provide structured, constructive feedback to their peers using both L1 and English as appropriate for comprehension and communication. Students learn specific feedback protocols, use sentence frames for academic language practice, and engage in peer discussions that may include L1 processing before providing English feedback. This approach develops critical thinking, communication skills, and metacognitive awareness while providing multiple perspectives on learning. Teachers model respectful feedback practices and provide scaffolds for both giving and receiving feedback. The strategy is particularly effective in writing workshops, project presentations, and collaborative learning activities where peer input enhances the learning process.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Structured feedback protocols and sentence frames for academic language
- L1 discussion opportunities before English feedback provision
- Respectful, constructive feedback practices modeled and practiced
- Student development of critical thinking and communication skills

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Unstructured peer feedback without clear protocols or language support
- Feedback that focuses on surface-level corrections without deeper learning
- Peer assessment without teaching students how to give constructive feedback

### Strategy ID: alternative_assessment

**Name:** Alternative Assessment Methods

**Aliases:** Performance-based assessment, Authentic assessment

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** speaking, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.3; listening: 0.2; reading: 0.2; writing: 0.3

**Delivery Mode:** individual, small-group, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, none

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Provide multiple ways for students to demonstrate knowledge that don't rely heavily on English proficiency.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content area assessment
- Accommodating diverse learning styles
- Reducing language barriers in assessment

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Design assessments using visuals, demonstrations, and hands-on activities.
2. Allow choice in assessment format and language use when appropriate.
3. Focus on content knowledge rather than English language production.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Provides equitable access to demonstrate learning
- Reduces language barriers in content assessment

**Research Foundation:** Grant Wiggins, Jay McTighe, Jamal Abedi

**Cross-References:** differentiated_instruction, formative_assessment_ml

**Short Definition:**
Assessment approaches that provide multiple ways for students to demonstrate knowledge without heavy reliance on English proficiency.

**Long Definition:**
Alternative Assessment Methods involve designing assessment approaches that provide multiple ways for multilingual learners to demonstrate their knowledge and skills without heavy reliance on English language proficiency. These methods include performance-based assessments, visual demonstrations, hands-on projects, oral presentations with visual supports, and technology-enhanced assessments. Students may be given choices in assessment format and appropriate language use to show content mastery. The focus shifts from English language production to authentic demonstration of learning objectives. Alternative assessments often mirror real-world applications and provide more equitable access for students at various English proficiency levels. Implementation requires careful alignment with learning objectives while removing unnecessary language barriers that might mask content knowledge.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Multiple assessment formats accommodating different learning styles and language levels
- Focus on content knowledge demonstration rather than English production
- Visual, hands-on, and performance-based assessment options
- Student choice in assessment format when appropriate

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Only traditional paper-and-pencil tests requiring high English proficiency
- Assessment methods that confuse language ability with content knowledge
- One-size-fits-all assessments without consideration of language barriers

### Strategy ID: language_objectives_assessment

**Name:** Language Objectives Assessment

**Aliases:** Academic language assessment, Language function evaluation

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Assessment & Scaffolding, Content & Language Integration

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** speaking, writing, reading, listening

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Systematically assess students' development of academic language functions and structures.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content and language integrated lessons
- Academic language development tracking
- WIDA standards alignment

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Identify specific language functions and structures for assessment.
2. Use rubrics that separate content knowledge from language proficiency.
3. Provide feedback on both content mastery and language development.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Tracks academic language development systematically
- Informs targeted language instruction

**Research Foundation:** Pauline Gibbons, Margo Gottlieb, WIDA Consortium

**Cross-References:** siop, clil, formative_assessment_ml

**Short Definition:**
Systematic assessment of students' development in academic language functions and structures aligned with content learning.

**Long Definition:**
Language Objectives Assessment involves systematically evaluating students' development of specific academic language functions, structures, and vocabulary within content area learning. Teachers identify target language objectives (such as comparing, explaining, arguing) and assess student progress using rubrics that separate content knowledge from language proficiency development. This approach aligns with frameworks like WIDA standards and SIOP methodology, providing specific feedback on both content mastery and language growth. Assessment methods include structured observations, language-focused rubrics, and documentation of student language use in academic contexts. The goal is to track academic language development over time while informing targeted language instruction that supports content learning objectives.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Clear identification of specific language functions and structures for assessment
- Rubrics that separate content knowledge from language proficiency
- Systematic tracking of academic language development over time
- Targeted feedback on both content and language objectives

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Assessment that only focuses on content without attention to language development
- Vague language objectives without specific assessment criteria
- Confusing content mastery with English language proficiency levels
