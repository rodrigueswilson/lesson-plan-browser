### Strategy ID: bilingual_dictionary

**Name:** Bilingual Dictionary and Glossary Use

**Aliases:** Bilingual glossaries

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Language Skill Development, Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** reading

**Secondary Skills:** writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0; listening: 0; reading: 0.5; writing: 0.5

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, small-group, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Leverage L1–L2 dictionaries to clarify new terms and support independent comprehension.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Self-directed reading or research
- Content lessons with dense academic vocabulary
- Writing tasks requiring precise word choice

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Model context-first inference, then confirm with a bilingual dictionary.
2. Embed routine look-ups in reading/writing; teach efficient dictionary skills.
3. Verify fit-in-context (e.g., reverse-translation or image check).

**Primary Benefits:**
- Improves comprehension of academic texts
- Builds bilingual vocabulary and learner autonomy

**Research Foundation:** Jim Cummins, Ellen Bialystok

**Cross-References:** explicit_vocabulary, cognate_awareness

**Short Definition:**
Strategic use of bilingual dictionaries and glossaries to support vocabulary development and reading comprehension.

**Long Definition:**
Bilingual Dictionary and Glossary Use involves teaching students to strategically leverage L1–L2 dictionaries and specialized glossaries to clarify unfamiliar terms and support independent comprehension. Teachers model a context-first approach where students first attempt to infer meaning from surrounding text before confirming with dictionary resources. This strategy emphasizes efficient dictionary skills including understanding multiple definitions, checking word forms, and verifying contextual appropriateness through reverse-translation or visual confirmation. Students learn to integrate dictionary use into their reading and writing routines, building both bilingual vocabulary and learner autonomy. The approach supports academic language development while honoring students' L1 as a cognitive resource for English learning.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Students attempting context clues before dictionary consultation
- Efficient dictionary navigation and multiple definition consideration
- Verification of word meaning through reverse-translation or images
- Integration of new vocabulary into speaking and writing tasks

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Immediate dictionary dependence without context consideration
- Using only monolingual English dictionaries with beginning learners
- Dictionary lookup without checking contextual appropriateness

### Strategy ID: bilingual_word_walls

**Name:** Bilingual Word Walls and Labels

**Aliases:** L1–L2 classroom labels

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Language Skill Development

**Primary Skill:** reading

**Secondary Skills:** speaking, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0; reading: 0.5; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Display key terms in English and L1 to make vocabulary constantly visible and meaningful.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Vocabulary-heavy units
- Literacy centers/emergent readers
- Thematic launches

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Co-create bilingual word cards with visuals; organize by theme or alphabet.
2. Routinely reference during instruction and practice via quick games.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Reinforces retention through exposure
- Builds inclusion and independence

**Research Foundation:** Jim Cummins, Joanna Conteh

**Cross-References:** explicit_vocabulary, graphic_organizers

**Short Definition:**
Dual-language word walls and labels that pair key terms with visuals to make academic vocabulary continually visible and usable.

**Long Definition:**
Bilingual Word Walls and Labels display high-utility academic and content-specific terms in English alongside students' L1 equivalents, often with visuals, morphology cues, or example frames. Teachers co-create the wall with students, adding words during unit launches and revisiting it daily in mini-routines (quick reads, choral responses, sort-and-match, or 'use-it-in-a-sentence'). Cards may be organized by theme, concept map, or part of speech; color-coding highlights cognates or affixes. During lessons, teachers point to the wall to prompt accurate retrieval, help students choose precise terms in speaking and writing, and reinforce spelling and pronunciation. Because the wall lives in the room, it supports independence, inclusion, and repeated exposure—key drivers of vocabulary growth. Use it in vocabulary-heavy units and literacy centers; keep it curated and legible. Avoid cluttered, static displays that are never referenced; the wall must be interactive and aligned to current learning goals to remain effective.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Co-created bilingual cards with visuals or morphology cues
- Daily reference during instruction and practice
- Organized, current terms tied to the unit focus
- Students using wall terms in speaking and writing

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- A dense, outdated wall never referenced in lessons
- Random long lists with no visuals or organization
- Using only English labels in bilingual classrooms

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Systematically pre-teach essential vocabulary with multiple exposures and meaningful practice.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content-heavy lessons with tier 2/3 vocabulary
- Academic reading preparation
- Writing instruction requiring precise terminology

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Select 3-5 essential terms; provide student-friendly definitions with examples.
2. Use multiple modalities (visual, kinesthetic, linguistic) for initial instruction.
3. Provide structured practice opportunities and assess understanding.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Removes vocabulary barriers to comprehension
- Builds academic language systematically

**Research Foundation:** Isabel Beck, Margaret McKeown, Linda Kucan

**Cross-References:** cognate_awareness, bilingual_dictionary, graphic_organizers

**Short Definition:**
Systematic pre-teaching of essential vocabulary using multiple modalities and structured practice opportunities.

**Long Definition:**
Explicit Vocabulary Instruction involves the systematic pre-teaching of essential academic and content-specific vocabulary through direct, intentional instruction. Teachers select 3-5 high-utility terms that are crucial for lesson comprehension, providing student-friendly definitions, examples, and non-examples. Instruction incorporates multiple modalities including visual representations, kinesthetic activities, and linguistic connections. Students engage in structured practice through activities like word sorts, semantic mapping, and contextual application. The approach emphasizes deep word knowledge rather than superficial exposure, including understanding of word relationships, multiple meanings, and appropriate usage contexts. For multilingual learners, explicit vocabulary instruction often includes cognate connections, L1 translations, and cultural context explanations to build bridges between languages and enhance comprehension.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Pre-selection of 3-5 essential vocabulary terms
- Student-friendly definitions with clear examples and non-examples
- Multiple modality instruction (visual, kinesthetic, linguistic)
- Structured practice activities and comprehension checks

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Introducing too many vocabulary words at once
- Providing only dictionary definitions without examples
- One-time vocabulary introduction without follow-up practice

### Strategy ID: cognate_awareness

**Name:** Cognate Awareness and Instruction

**Aliases:** Cross-linguistic vocabulary connections

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Language Skill Development, Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** reading

**Secondary Skills:** writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.1; listening: 0.1; reading: 0.6; writing: 0.2

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, strategic-code-switch

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Leverage shared word roots between L1 and English to accelerate vocabulary acquisition.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Academic vocabulary instruction
- Reading comprehension support
- Content area learning with technical terms

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Identify true cognates in lesson vocabulary; teach recognition patterns.
2. Address false cognates explicitly; provide memory strategies.
3. Create cognate charts and encourage student discovery.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Accelerates vocabulary acquisition
- Builds metalinguistic awareness and confidence

**Research Foundation:** Jim Cummins, Diane August, Cheryl Dressler

**Cross-References:** explicit_vocabulary, contrastive_analysis, metalinguistic_awareness

**Short Definition:**
Strategic instruction in recognizing and using words that share similar forms and meanings across languages.

**Long Definition:**
Cognate Awareness and Instruction involves teaching students to recognize and strategically use words that share similar forms and meanings between their L1 and English. Teachers explicitly identify true cognates (words with shared etymology and meaning) and teach recognition patterns, such as common morphological transformations between languages. Instruction includes addressing false cognates (false friends) that share form but differ in meaning, providing explicit memory strategies to avoid confusion. Students learn to create cognate charts, develop cognate-hunting skills during reading, and use cognate knowledge to unlock unfamiliar vocabulary. This strategy is particularly powerful for students whose L1 shares Latin or Greek roots with English, as it leverages existing vocabulary knowledge to accelerate English acquisition while building metalinguistic awareness about language relationships.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Explicit identification and teaching of true cognates
- Recognition pattern instruction for morphological transformations
- False cognate awareness and memory strategies
- Student-generated cognate discoveries and charts

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Assuming all similar-looking words are cognates
- Ignoring false cognates that can cause confusion
- Teaching cognates without morphological pattern awareness

- Writing planning and organization

Teachers select organizers that match both the learning objective and the required language functions, modeling completion through think-alouds and providing sentence frames to support verbal processing. - Providing organizers without modeling or language support

**Primary Skill:** writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.3; listening: 0.1; reading: 0.3; writing: 0.3

**Delivery Mode:** whole-group, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Use students' experiences and oral language as the foundation for reading and writing instruction.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Beginning literacy instruction
- Personal narrative writing
- Content area experience documentation

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Facilitate shared experience or discussion of personal experiences.
2. Record student language verbatim, then use text for reading and language analysis.
3. Extend to individual writing and vocabulary development activities.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Connects oral and written language naturally
- Uses familiar content to support literacy development

**Research Foundation:** Roach Van Allen, MaryAnne Hall, Dorsey Hammond

**Cross-References:** interactive_read_alouds, sentence_frames, culturally_responsive_pedagogy

**Short Definition:**
Literacy instruction that uses students' own experiences and oral language as the foundation for reading and writing development.

**Long Definition:**
Language Experience Approach (LEA) is a literacy instruction method that uses students' own experiences and oral language as the foundation for reading and writing development. Teachers facilitate shared experiences or discussions about personal experiences, then record students' language verbatim to create authentic texts for reading instruction. These student-generated texts become the basis for vocabulary development, phonics instruction, and comprehension activities because the content is familiar and personally meaningful. Students see their own words and experiences valued in print, building confidence and understanding of the reading-writing connection. The approach is particularly effective for multilingual learners because it honors their experiences and language while providing scaffolded entry into English literacy. LEA texts can be revisited for language analysis, extended into individual writing projects, and used to build classroom libraries of student-authored materials.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Student experiences and oral language recorded verbatim
- Use of student-generated texts for reading instruction
- Extension to vocabulary development and language analysis
- Connection between oral language, reading, and writing made explicit

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Teacher-corrected or heavily edited student language
- Generic experiences not connected to students' lives
- One-time dictation without follow-up reading or analysis

- Frames provided without modeling or practice opportunities

### Strategy ID: total_physical_response

**Name:** Total Physical Response (TPR)

**Aliases:** Movement-based language learning, Kinesthetic language instruction

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Language Skill Development

**Primary Skill:** listening

**Secondary Skills:** speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.3; listening: 0.7; reading: 0; writing: 0

**Delivery Mode:** whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** none, translanguaging

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Use physical movement and gestures to support language comprehension and retention.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Beginning language learners
- Vocabulary introduction and practice
- Classroom management and routine instruction

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Model actions while speaking; have students respond physically before verbal production.
2. Progress from simple commands to complex sequences and storytelling.
3. Incorporate student-created movements and cultural gestures.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Reduces anxiety and supports comprehension
- Engages kinesthetic learners and aids memory

**Research Foundation:** James Asher, Stephen Krashen

**Cross-References:** comprehensible_input, culturally_responsive_pedagogy, interactive_read_alouds

**Short Definition:**
Language learning approach that uses physical movement and gestures to support comprehension and vocabulary acquisition.

**Long Definition:**
Total Physical Response (TPR) is a language learning approach that uses physical movement and gestures to support comprehension and vocabulary acquisition. Teachers model actions while speaking, allowing students to respond physically before requiring verbal production, which reduces anxiety and supports natural language acquisition processes. The method progresses from simple commands to complex sequences and storytelling, engaging kinesthetic learners while creating memorable associations between language and movement. TPR is particularly effective for beginning language learners and vocabulary introduction because it provides comprehensible input through multiple modalities. Effective implementation incorporates student-created movements, cultural gestures, and connections to academic content. The approach honors the silent period in language acquisition while building confidence and comprehension through meaningful physical engagement with language.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Physical modeling of actions while speaking
- Student physical responses before verbal production required
- Progression from simple commands to complex sequences
- Integration of student-created movements and cultural gestures

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Requiring immediate verbal responses without physical comprehension
- Using movements that are culturally inappropriate or embarrassing
- Limiting TPR to isolated vocabulary without meaningful contexts
