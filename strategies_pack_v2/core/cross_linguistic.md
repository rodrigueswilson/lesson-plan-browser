### Strategy ID: the_bridge

**Name:** The Bridge (Cross-Language Transfer)

**Aliases:** Biliteracy Bridge

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** reading

**Secondary Skills:** writing, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.2; listening: 0.1; reading: 0.4; writing: 0.3

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, small-group, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Explicitly map learned content/vocabulary from L1 to English (and vice versa) to ensure transfer.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Dual-language units
- Push-in review after English lessons

**Implementation Steps:**
1. List key L1 concepts; co-create English equivalents in two-column charts.
2. Compare cognates/false cognates; apply via English summaries/presentations.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Prevents compartmentalization
- Builds biliterate academic vocabulary

**Research Foundation:** Karen Beeman, Cheryl Urow

**Cross-References:** cognate_awareness, preview_review

**Short Definition:**
Systematic process for explicitly connecting learning between L1 and English to ensure cross-linguistic transfer.

**Long Definition:**
The Bridge is a systematic instructional process that explicitly connects learning between students' L1 and English to ensure cross-linguistic transfer and prevent compartmentalization of knowledge. Teachers guide students to map learned content and vocabulary from one language to another using visual organizers, two-column charts, and comparative analysis. The process includes identifying cognates and false cognates, comparing linguistic structures, and applying transferred knowledge through English summaries or presentations. This strategy is particularly powerful in dual-language programs where students learn content in both languages. The Bridge helps students see connections between their languages, builds metalinguistic awareness, and develops biliterate academic vocabulary. Implementation requires careful planning to ensure meaningful connections and authentic application opportunities in both languages.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Two-column charts mapping L1 and English concepts/vocabulary
- Explicit comparison of cognates and false cognates
- Student application through English summaries or presentations
- Systematic transfer activities connecting both languages

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Simple translation without conceptual connections
- Separate instruction in each language without bridging
- Random code-switching without systematic transfer focus

### Strategy ID: strategic_code_switching

**Name:** Strategic Code-Switching

**Aliases:** Planned L1 clarification

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** speaking

**Secondary Skills:** listening

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.6; listening: 0.4; reading: 0; writing: 0

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** strategic-code-switch

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Brief, purposeful switches to L1 to unlock comprehension, then immediate return to English.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Complex concepts/terms
- Task directions/safety info

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Clarify in L1 in a sentence or two; immediately restate in English.
2. Monitor frequency and reduce as proficiency grows.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Prevents misunderstandings and frustration
- Supports confidence and efficiency

**Research Foundation:** M. Sakaria, Dwi Priyana

**Cross-References:** preview_review, translanguaging

**Short Definition:**
Purposeful, brief switches to L1 for clarification followed by immediate return to English instruction.

**Long Definition:**
Strategic Code-Switching involves purposeful, brief switches to students' L1 to clarify complex concepts, vocabulary, or instructions, followed by immediate return to English. This approach differs from random code-switching by being planned, limited, and goal-oriented. Teachers use L1 strategically for critical comprehension points, safety information, or complex academic concepts that might otherwise create barriers to learning. The key is brevity and immediate bridging back to English to maintain English exposure while preventing frustration and misunderstanding. Frequency should decrease as student proficiency grows. This strategy requires careful monitoring to ensure it supports rather than replaces English development. When used judiciously, strategic code-switching can unlock comprehension and build confidence while maintaining focus on English acquisition goals.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Brief, purposeful L1 use for critical comprehension points
- Immediate restatement or return to English after L1 clarification
- Strategic timing for complex concepts or safety information
- Decreasing frequency as student proficiency develops

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Extensive L1 use that replaces English instruction
- Random code-switching without clear purpose or planning
- L1 use without bridging back to English learning objectives

### Strategy ID: preview_review

**Name:** Preview-Review

**Aliases:** L1 preview, English instruction, L1 review

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** listening

**Secondary Skills:** reading, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.2; listening: 0.5; reading: 0.2; writing: 0.1

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group

**L1 Mode:** preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Preview content in L1, teach in English, then review/consolidate in L1.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content-heavy lessons
- New concept introduction

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Brief L1 preview of key concepts and vocabulary.
2. Deliver main instruction in English with scaffolds.
3. L1 review to consolidate understanding and check comprehension.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Builds background knowledge and reduces cognitive load
- Ensures comprehension while maintaining English focus

**Research Foundation:** Jim Cummins, Nancy Cloud

**Cross-References:** strategic_code_switching, the_bridge

**Short Definition:**
Three-phase instructional sequence: L1 preview, English instruction, L1 review for comprehension consolidation.

**Long Definition:**
Preview-Review is a structured instructional sequence that uses students' L1 strategically to support English content learning. The three phases include: (1) L1 Preview - brief introduction of key concepts and vocabulary in students' native language to activate background knowledge, (2) English Instruction - main lesson delivery in English with appropriate scaffolds and supports, and (3) L1 Review - consolidation and comprehension check in L1 to ensure understanding. This approach reduces cognitive load by providing conceptual foundation in L1 while maintaining focus on English development during core instruction. The strategy is particularly effective for content-heavy lessons and complex concept introduction. Implementation requires careful time management to ensure the majority of instruction remains in English while using L1 strategically for comprehension support.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Brief L1 preview activating background knowledge
- Majority of instruction delivered in English with scaffolds
- L1 review consolidating understanding and checking comprehension
- Clear transitions between L1 and English phases

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Extended L1 instruction that replaces English learning
- Random language switching without structured sequence
- L1 preview or review that becomes the main instruction

### Strategy ID: contrastive_analysis

**Name:** Contrastive Analysis

**Aliases:** Cross-linguistic comparison

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy

**Primary Skill:** reading

**Secondary Skills:** writing, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.2; listening: 0.1; reading: 0.4; writing: 0.3

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, strategic-code-switch

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Compare L1 and English structures to anticipate challenges and highlight transferable knowledge.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Grammar instruction
- Phonological awareness
- Writing conventions

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Present side-by-side L1-English examples on board or chart.
2. Discuss both transferable features and potential pitfalls.
3. Have students practice transforming sentences across languages.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Builds metalinguistic awareness
- Prevents fossilization of common errors

**Research Foundation:** Robert Lado, Terence Odlin

**Cross-References:** cognate_awareness, metalinguistic_awareness

**Short Definition:**
Teaching students to compare L1 and English structures to anticipate challenges and highlight transferable knowledge.

**Long Definition:**
Contrastive Analysis is the systematic comparison of linguistic structures in students' L1 and in English to highlight similarities and differences. Teachers guide students to notice phonological, morphological, syntactic, or pragmatic features that transfer positively (e.g., cognates, similar word order) and those that may cause errors (e.g., false cognates, article use). In practice, teachers present side‑by‑side examples, chart similarities, and explicitly point out potential pitfalls. Students practice transforming sentences across languages and reflect on how knowledge of L1 helps anticipate English patterns. This fosters metalinguistic awareness and prevents fossilization of common errors. Contrastive analysis is especially effective when introducing complex grammar, writing conventions, or academic registers. It should not devolve into prescriptive 'avoid mistakes' lists; instead, it should celebrate cross‑linguistic resources while clarifying non‑equivalences.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Side‑by‑side L1–English examples on board or chart
- Discussion of both transferable features and pitfalls
- Students transforming or translating short sentences
- Reflection on how L1 knowledge informs English learning

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Only error‑correction without positive transfer
- Focusing exclusively on L1 interference as deficit
- Teaching rules in isolation without contrast

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** No

**Core Principle:**
Use texts available in both L1 and English to support comprehension and cross-linguistic connections.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Literature study
- Content area reading
- Independent reading programs

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Select high-quality texts available in both languages.
2. Allow students to read in L1 first, then English version.
3. Facilitate discussions comparing language use and cultural elements.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Supports comprehension through L1 foundation
- Develops biliteracy and cultural connections

**Research Foundation:** Alma Flor Ada, Isabel Campoy

**Cross-References:** interactive_read_alouds, culturally_responsive_pedagogy

**Short Definition:**
Use of texts available in both L1 and English to support comprehension and develop biliteracy skills.

**Long Definition:**
Bilingual Books and Parallel Texts strategy involves using high-quality literature and informational texts that are available in both students' L1 and English to support reading comprehension and biliteracy development. Students may read the L1 version first to build comprehension and background knowledge, then engage with the English version with enhanced understanding. This approach allows for rich discussions comparing language use, cultural elements, and translation choices. Teachers can facilitate cross-linguistic analysis, vocabulary development, and cultural connections. The strategy is particularly effective for building confidence in English reading while honoring students' L1 literacy skills. Implementation requires careful text selection to ensure quality in both languages and meaningful opportunities for comparison and analysis.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- High-quality texts available in both L1 and English
- Strategic sequencing of L1 and English reading
- Cross-linguistic discussions and comparisons
- Cultural connections and identity affirmation

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Poor quality translations that don't maintain meaning
- Using only L1 texts without English connection
- Literal word-for-word translations without cultural adaptation

**Applicable Contexts:**

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Engage students in explicit discussion about language patterns and rules.
2. Encourage reflection on language learning strategies and processes.
3. Compare language structures and functions across L1 and English.

**Primary Benefits:**

**Short Definition:**
Instruction that develops students' conscious awareness of language as a system and ability to reflect on language use.

**Long Definition:**
Metalinguistic Awareness Development involves teaching students to think consciously about language as a system, including its patterns, rules, and functions. Students learn to reflect on their own language use, compare languages, and analyze linguistic structures and processes. This awareness includes understanding how languages work, recognizing patterns across languages, and developing strategic approaches to language learning. Teachers engage students in explicit discussions about grammar, vocabulary, discourse patterns, and language learning strategies. For multilingual learners, metalinguistic awareness is particularly powerful because it helps them leverage knowledge across languages and become more strategic language learners. This approach enhances both language learning efficiency and critical thinking about communication.
