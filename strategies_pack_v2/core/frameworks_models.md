### Strategy ID: translanguaging

**Name:** Translanguaging

**Aliases:** Flexible bilingual pedagogy

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Cross-Linguistic & Biliteracy, Instructional Frameworks & Models

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** speaking, listening, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Invite students' full linguistic repertoire for meaning-making and expression.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content instruction with multilinguals
- Brainstorming/drafting in L1 before English output

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Offer multilingual resources; allow L1 brainstorming/notes; peer translation tasks.
2. Bridge to English products with scaffolds and modeling.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Affirms identity and increases access
- Develops cognitive flexibility

**Research Foundation:** Ofelia García, Li Wei, Tatyana Kleyn, Nelson Flores

**Cross-References:** language_separation, collaborative_learning, the_bridge

**Short Definition:**
Pedagogy that allows learners to flexibly use their full linguistic repertoires (L1 and English) to learn and express meaning.

**Long Definition:**
Translanguaging is a teaching approach that legitimizes and strategically incorporates students' full linguistic repertoires into classroom learning. It allows learners to use both their L1 and English fluidly for meaning-making, collaboration, and identity expression. In practice, teachers may encourage brainstorming or drafting in L1, peer discussions in mixed languages, or bilingual word walls and resources. Students then bridge back to English for formal outputs, such as essays or presentations. This approach deepens comprehension, affirms cultural identity, and develops metalinguistic awareness. Translanguaging is not the same as random code-switching; it is purposeful, designed with clear objectives, and linked to outcomes in English. Overuse without bridging risks limiting English exposure; the key is balance and intentionality. When implemented thoughtfully, translanguaging transforms bilingualism from a challenge into a resource for learning.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Students using L1 resources to support English tasks
- Bilingual displays and word walls in classroom
- Teacher prompts bridging L1 work to English output
- Peer discussions leveraging both languages

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Teacher translating everything directly into L1
- Students using only L1 without bridging to English
- No clear objective guiding multilingual use

**Name:** SIOP (Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol)

**Aliases:** Sheltered instruction

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Instructional Frameworks & Models

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** listening, reading, writing, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review, strategic-code-switch

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Deliver grade-level content with language scaffolds, interaction, and comprehensible input.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Content classes with ELs
- Mixed proficiency classrooms

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Set content + language objectives; use visuals, modeling, and interaction.
2. Check comprehension frequently; provide meaningful practice with feedback.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Academic achievement in content areas
- Simultaneous language and content development

**Research Foundation:** Jana Echevarria, Deborah Short, MaryEllen Vogt

**Cross-References:** clil, scaffolding, formative_assessment

**Short Definition:**
Research-based framework for delivering grade-level content with systematic language scaffolds and comprehensible input.

**Long Definition:**
SIOP (Sheltered Instruction Observation Protocol) is a comprehensive framework for making grade-level academic content comprehensible to English learners while developing their English language skills. The model includes eight components: lesson preparation, building background, comprehensible input, strategies, interaction, practice and application, lesson delivery, and review and assessment. Teachers set both content and language objectives, use visuals and modeling, provide structured interaction opportunities, and check comprehension frequently. SIOP emphasizes meaningful practice with immediate feedback and connects new learning to students' background knowledge. The framework ensures that English learners have access to rigorous academic content while receiving the language support they need to succeed. Implementation requires systematic planning and attention to both content mastery and language development goals.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Clear content and language objectives posted and reviewed
- Systematic use of visuals, modeling, and hands-on activities
- Structured interaction opportunities and comprehension checks
- Meaningful practice with immediate feedback and review

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Content delivery without language objectives or scaffolds
- Lecture-heavy instruction without interaction or visuals
- Assessment without consideration of language proficiency levels

### Strategy ID: clil

**Name:** Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL)

**Aliases:** Content-Based Instruction (CBI)

**Present In:** ChatGPT, Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Instructional Frameworks & Models, Content & Language Integration

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** listening, reading, writing, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Plan for simultaneous content objectives and language objectives with scaffolding.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Subject-area classes
- Immersion programs

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Identify content + language goals; scaffold with visuals, L1 support, and interaction.
2. Assess both content mastery and language growth.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Authentic language use in meaningful contexts
- Efficient dual learning

**Research Foundation:** Do Coyle, Philip Hood, David Marsh

**Cross-References:** SIOP, Task-Based Learning

**Short Definition:**
Educational approach that integrates content learning with language development through meaningful, contextualized instruction.

**Long Definition:**
Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL) is an educational approach where subjects are taught through a foreign or second language, with dual objectives of content mastery and language development. The framework emphasizes authentic language use in meaningful academic contexts, where students learn language through content rather than studying language in isolation. CLIL lessons integrate the 4Cs: Content (subject matter), Communication (language skills), Cognition (thinking skills), and Culture (awareness and understanding). Teachers plan lessons with explicit content and language objectives, using scaffolding techniques such as visuals, graphic organizers, and L1 support when appropriate. Assessment addresses both content knowledge and language proficiency growth. This approach is particularly effective because it provides natural, purposeful contexts for language use while maintaining academic rigor in content areas.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Explicit content and language objectives in lesson planning
- Authentic use of academic language in content contexts
- Integration of the 4Cs: Content, Communication, Cognition, Culture
- Assessment of both content mastery and language development

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Teaching content and language as separate, unconnected subjects
- Focus only on content without attention to language development
- Language instruction without meaningful content contexts

**Present In:** ChatGPT

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Instructional Frameworks & Models, Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** speaking, listening, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review, strategic-code-switch

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Teach learning strategies (metacognitive, cognitive, social) alongside content.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Upper-elementary and secondary content classes
- Lessons requiring complex texts/problem solving

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Set content + strategy goals; model with think-alouds.
2. Guide practice (e.g., summarizing, graphic organizers) and reflect on transfer.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Builds metacognition and academic language
- Increases learner autonomy

**Research Foundation:** Anna Uhl Chamot, J. Michael O'Malley

**Cross-References:** siop, metacognitive_strategies

**Short Definition:**
Framework that explicitly teaches learning strategies alongside academic content to develop both language and cognitive skills.

**Long Definition:**
CALLA (Cognitive Academic Language Learning Approach) is an instructional framework that explicitly teaches learning strategies alongside academic content to help students develop both language proficiency and cognitive academic skills. The model focuses on three types of strategies: metacognitive (planning, monitoring, evaluating), cognitive (note-taking, summarizing, inferencing), and social/affective (cooperation, questioning for clarification). Teachers model strategy use through think-alouds, provide guided practice opportunities, and help students reflect on strategy effectiveness and transfer. CALLA lessons integrate content objectives with strategy objectives, ensuring that students not only learn subject matter but also develop the tools for independent learning. This approach is particularly effective for intermediate and advanced English learners who need to develop academic language and study skills for success in content area classes.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Explicit strategy instruction integrated with content learning
- Teacher modeling of metacognitive, cognitive, and social strategies
- Student reflection on strategy use and effectiveness
- Transfer of strategies across different content areas and contexts

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Teaching strategies in isolation without content connection
- Assuming students will naturally develop learning strategies
- Focus only on content without attention to learning processes

**Aliases:** Flexible grouping & tasks

**Present In:** Gemini

**Evidence Tier:** practice-based/mixed

**High-Level Categories:** Instructional Frameworks & Models, Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** listening, reading, writing, speaking

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Adjust content, process, and product based on proficiency, interests, and readiness.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Classes with wide proficiency ranges
- Universal access to grade-level content

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Use formative data to group; provide multiple pathways/materials.
2. Integrate scaffolds, translanguaging, and varied outputs.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Equity of access
- Higher engagement and belonging

**Research Foundation:** Carol Ann Tomlinson, Diane August

**Cross-References:** formative_assessment, scaffolding

**Short Definition:**
Instructional approach that adjusts content, process, and products based on student readiness, interests, and learning profiles.

**Long Definition:**
Differentiated Instruction is a framework for teaching that recognizes and responds to student diversity in readiness levels, interests, and learning preferences. Teachers proactively modify content (what students learn), process (how they learn), and products (how they demonstrate learning) to ensure all students can access grade-level curriculum. For multilingual learners, differentiation includes language scaffolds, culturally relevant materials, flexible grouping strategies, and varied assessment options. Teachers use ongoing formative assessment to make instructional decisions and adjust support levels. The approach emphasizes student choice and voice while maintaining high expectations for all learners. Effective differentiation for English learners integrates translanguaging opportunities, visual supports, and multiple ways to demonstrate understanding while building toward English proficiency goals.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Flexible grouping based on formative assessment data
- Multiple pathways to access the same learning objectives
- Varied materials and resources at different complexity levels
- Choice in how students demonstrate their learning

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Same instruction and materials for all students regardless of needs
- Lowered expectations or watered-down content for English learners
- Permanent ability grouping without opportunities for movement

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Engage students in authentic, extended projects that integrate content learning with real-world problem solving.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- Interdisciplinary units
- Community-connected learning
- Extended inquiry projects

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Design authentic driving questions connected to standards.
2. Provide scaffolds for research, collaboration, and presentation.
3. Include multiple checkpoints and peer feedback opportunities.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Develops 21st century skills and deep content knowledge
- Provides authentic contexts for language use

**Research Foundation:** John Dewey, William Kilpatrick, Buck Institute for Education

**Cross-References:** collaborative_learning, authentic_assessment, inquiry_based_learning

**Short Definition:**
Extended learning experiences where students investigate authentic problems and create meaningful products for real audiences.

**Long Definition:**
Project-Based Learning (PBL) is an instructional methodology where students learn by actively engaging in authentic, complex projects over extended periods. Students investigate driving questions, conduct research, collaborate with peers, and create products or solutions for real audiences. PBL integrates multiple content areas and develops both academic knowledge and 21st-century skills such as critical thinking, collaboration, and communication. For multilingual learners, PBL provides natural contexts for language development through meaningful interaction, research, and presentation opportunities. Teachers act as facilitators, providing scaffolds, checkpoints, and feedback throughout the process. Projects often connect to community issues or student interests, increasing engagement and relevance. Assessment is ongoing and multifaceted, including self-reflection, peer feedback, and authentic evaluation of final products.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Authentic driving questions connected to real-world problems
- Extended timeline with multiple checkpoints and revisions
- Student voice and choice in project direction and products
- Collaboration, research, and presentation opportunities

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Short-term activities or crafts without authentic purpose
- Teacher-directed projects with predetermined outcomes
- Projects without connection to academic standards or real-world relevance

### Strategy ID: scaffolded_instruction

**Name:** Scaffolded Instruction

**Aliases:** Gradual release of responsibility, I do, we do, you do

**Present In:** v1.4 enhancement

**Evidence Tier:** research-backed

**High-Level Categories:** Instructional Frameworks & Models, Assessment & Scaffolding

**Primary Skill:** integrated

**Secondary Skills:** listening, speaking, reading, writing

**Skill Weights:** speaking: 0.25; listening: 0.25; reading: 0.25; writing: 0.25

**Delivery Mode:** push-in, whole-group, small-group, individual

**L1 Mode:** translanguaging, preview-review

**Integrated Framework:** Yes

**Core Principle:**
Provide temporary supports that are gradually removed as students develop independence.

**Applicable Contexts:**
- New skill introduction
- Complex task completion
- Language development activities

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Model the skill or strategy with explicit instruction.
2. Provide guided practice with decreasing support.
3. Monitor independent practice and provide feedback.

**Primary Benefits:**
- Builds student confidence and competence
- Supports successful completion of challenging tasks

**Research Foundation:** Lev Vygotsky, Jerome Bruner, Pauline Gibbons

**Cross-References:** zone_of_proximal_development, gradual_release, modeling

**Short Definition:**
Instructional technique that provides temporary supports which are gradually removed as students develop independence.

**Long Definition:**
Scaffolded Instruction is based on Vygotsky's concept of the Zone of Proximal Development, providing temporary supports that enable students to accomplish tasks they cannot yet complete independently. The process follows a gradual release of responsibility model: teacher modeling (I do), guided practice (we do), and independent practice (you do). Scaffolds can be procedural (step-by-step guides), strategic (thinking processes), or linguistic (sentence frames, vocabulary support). For multilingual learners, scaffolds often include visual supports, L1 connections, peer collaboration, and modified language complexity. The key is to provide just enough support for success while building toward independence. Effective scaffolding requires ongoing assessment to determine when supports can be reduced or removed. The goal is to develop autonomous learners who can transfer skills and strategies to new contexts.

**Look-Fors (Observable Indicators):**
- Clear modeling and explicit instruction of new skills
- Guided practice with decreasing levels of support
- Multiple types of scaffolds (visual, linguistic, procedural)
- Systematic removal of supports as students gain competence

**Non-Examples (Avoid):**
- Providing supports indefinitely without building independence
- Removing scaffolds too quickly before students are ready
- One-size-fits-all supports without consideration of individual needs
