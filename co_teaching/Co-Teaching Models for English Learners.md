

# **Maximizing Instructional Intensity for Multilingual Learners: A Research-Informed Analysis of Friend and Cook Co-Teaching Models**

## **Executive Summary: Policy Implications and Model Selection for Multilingual Learners**

The expert analysis of co-teaching literature regarding English Language Learners (ELLs), increasingly referred to as Multilingual Learners (MLs), indicates that the effectiveness of the six Friend and Cook co-teaching models is fundamentally contingent upon **implementation fidelity** and the administrative provision of dedicated resources, rather than the intrinsic superiority of any single model structure. High-fidelity practice requires continuous adherence to cycles of collaboration, specifically Co-planning, Co-teaching, Co-assessing, and Co-reflecting, as defined by frameworks such as the WIDA English Language Development (ELD) Standards.1

A persistent concern across the field is the violation of the **equity mandate**. Research highlights that routine reliance on low-intensity models, such as One Teach, One Assist, is a critical failure factor that restricts EL access to grade-level content and the content expert's instructional skills.2 For co-teaching to be sustainable and successful, schools must establish protected, dedicated co-planning time, moving beyond a logistical minimum of 20 minutes per week 3 toward the intensive planning required for complex dual-language coordination, often documented as requiring a 90-minute weekly block.4 Model selection must be strategic and differentiated based on WIDA English proficiency levels, reserving high-intensity models—such as Parallel, Station, and Team Teaching—for all students, and carefully calibrating their use to match the linguistic needs of Beginning (Levels 1–2), Intermediate (Levels 3–4), and Advanced (Levels 5–6) learners.

## **I. The Effectiveness Debate: Research Synthesis on Academic and Language Outcomes**

### **1.1. Context and Current Status of Effectiveness Research (Post-2015)**

Co-teaching was originally conceptualized as a service delivery option to integrate students with disabilities (SWD) into mainstream classrooms, ensuring they received specialized instruction while maintaining access to the general curriculum.5 The integration of English Language Learners (ELs) into co-taught settings is a direct response to the long-standing recognition that traditional pullout models are less effective. Traditional pullout programs often create a significant disconnect between the isolated language instruction and the academic content presented in the mainstream classroom, hindering the MLs' ability to simultaneously acquire content and language.2

Regarding the specific effectiveness of the six Friend and Cook models (One Teach, One Observe; One Teach, One Assist; Parallel Teaching; Station Teaching; Alternative Teaching; Team Teaching) for improving ML academic and language outcomes, the existing body of post-2015 research, particularly meta-analyses, presents limited direct statistical evidence. Recent meta-analyses primarily concerning SWD outcomes report that co-teaching efficacy for overall academic achievement is still "up for debate," with studies showing "little statistical significance" for academic gains when co-teaching is implemented broadly.8 This limitation suggests that the

*structure* of the co-teaching model, when viewed in isolation, does not guarantee positive outcomes. Policy decisions must therefore be guided less by broad statistical claims of superiority and more by data showing fidelity of implementation and resulting equitable instructional practices.

### **1.2. Evaluation of Model Effectiveness by Instructional Intensity**

For MLs, effectiveness is directly correlated with models that mandate **shared responsibility and increased instructional intensity**. Models that facilitate smaller group sizes or require both teachers to actively engage in content delivery are associated with the strongest, albeit conditional, evidence of positive impact. These high-intensity models provide enhanced opportunities for complex language modeling, targeted language practice, and increased student participation.

The models demonstrating the highest potential for instructional intensity for MLs are **Station Teaching, Parallel Teaching, and Team Teaching**. Station Teaching is highly effective for delivering targeted language practice 6, Parallel Teaching reduces the student-to-teacher ratio, maximizing interaction and discourse, and Team Teaching provides expert, integrated modeling of content and academic language.

Conversely, models requiring less integrated effort carry high risks of failure and equity violation: **One Teach, One Assist** is the most problematic in the context of EL service delivery. Observational research in secondary settings has revealed that when co-teaching is poorly implemented, EL students tend to work primarily with the language specialist, often receiving only individualized support.2 This practice functions as the equivalent of the One Teach, One Assist model 9, which fundamentally violates the principle of parity central to co-teaching.7 In this arrangement, the general education teacher is effectively absolved of linguistic responsibility, and the language specialist is relegated to the role of an instructional aide.10 This functional degradation of roles destroys the core benefit of co-teaching—the integrated support of language and content—and ironically recreates the instructional isolation and disconnect that the original pullout model failure was meant to address.2

Table 1: Research-Based Effectiveness and Equity Risks of Friend and Cook Co-Teaching Models for ELLs

| Co-Teaching Model | Research Efficacy for Academic Gains (Conditional) | Required Planning Effort | Primary Equity/Fidelity Risk for ELLs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| One Teach, One Observe | Low/Informational (Data Collection) | Minimal (Focus on data points) | Specialist serves as passive data collector; high risk of non-parity. |

| Alternative Teaching | Moderate/Strong (For pre-/re-teaching) | Moderate (Requires selection of target group and specific activity design) | Risk of prolonged segregation or stigmatization of the target group. |
| Team Teaching | Moderate/Strong (For L2 modeling/integration) | High (Requires complete curriculum consensus and high trust) | Highly dependent on true parity and professional synergy. |

## **II. The Critical Gap: Limitations, Criticisms, and Failures in EL Co-Teaching**

### **2.1. The Fidelity Crisis and Equity Implications**

The most significant criticism of the Friend and Cook models, when applied to MLs, centers on the pervasive issue of fidelity. When co-teaching is implemented superficially, the critical objective of maximizing the Opportunity to Learn Content (OTL) for ELs is compromised.2 If the model defaults to a system where the ELD/ESL teacher primarily provides language interpretation, the EL student is implicitly denied full exposure to the content expert’s pedagogical methods and grade-level expectations.

The procedural failure of maintaining linguistic parity is tied directly to the instructional outcomes. Co-teaching is defined as a service delivery option requiring two or more professionals with equivalent licensure to share instructional responsibility.7 The functional failure occurs when the content teacher fails to integrate linguistic scaffolding and the language expert’s specialized knowledge is utilized only for classroom logistics, management, or minor assistance.9 The resultant restricted instructional intensity nullifies the educational benefit of having two professionals present, undermining the entire premise of the inclusive service model.11

### **2.2. Common Mistakes to Avoid**

Research into co-teaching failures has identified several recurring mistakes that diminish success and sustainability, particularly after the initial enthusiasm for the program fades.11

1. **Failure to Define and Share Understanding:** A foundational mistake is failing to define co-teaching clearly across all stakeholders, including teachers and administrators, thereby preventing consensus on core characteristics like mutual ownership, pooled resources, and joint accountability.11  
2. **Overuse of Low-Intensity Models:** Professionals often overuse models that require minimal planning or collaboration, such as One Teach, One Assist. This results in students not receiving the increased instructional intensity they are entitled to, effectively wasting the resource of having two teachers.11  
3. **Inappropriate Student Placement:** Co-teaching programs frequently fail by placing a disproportionate number of students with complex needs (including large numbers of high-need, Levels 1-2 ELs) into a co-taught class. This practice inadvertently turns the inclusion setting into a de facto segregated, self-contained classroom, overwhelming the teachers and diluting the ability to serve the entire group effectively.11  
4. **Time Evasion:** Administrative or teacher resistance often cites the lack of time as the primary reason for non-implementation. Using "time" as an excuse rather than proactively finding alternative logistical solutions (e.g., covering classes, utilizing grants for substitute time, or structuring independent work at the start of class for informal planning) guarantees failure.11

### **2.3. Recommended Alternative Approaches and Improvements**

The most critical recommendation for improving the effectiveness and fidelity of co-teaching for MLs is to shift the conceptual framework away from six static structures toward a dynamic, cyclical process. This model is embodied by the **WIDA Collaboration Cycle**, which comprises Co-planning, Co-teaching, Co-assessing, and Co-reflecting.1

This procedural shift forces collaboration to be focused on actionable, measurable outcomes. For instance, collaborative planning must utilize the WIDA Can Do Descriptors to strategically group students, monitor language growth during Co-assessing, and engage in meaningful reflection on shared instructional impact during Co-reflecting.1 Additionally, newer frameworks often emphasize instructional purpose, proposing alternative approaches such as "Two Teach Same Content," ensuring both experts are actively leading learning, rather than passively supporting.1

## **III. Strategic Model Selection by English Proficiency Level (WIDA Alignment)**

### **3.1. Differentiation Framework: The WIDA Standards and Can Do Descriptors**

The efficacy of a co-teaching model for MLs is maximized when it is strategically chosen to align with the students’ current linguistic and content needs. This differentiation framework is structured by the WIDA Language Expectations and the **WIDA Can Do Descriptors**.12 These tools enable educators to understand what students at various proficiency levels can reasonably achieve with language, allowing co-teachers to collaboratively design appropriate scaffolding, differentiate instruction, and ensure simultaneous content and language development.1

Table 2 illustrates the strategic model selection based on WIDA proficiency levels:

Table 2: Strategic Model Selection Based on WIDA English Proficiency Levels

| WIDA Proficiency Level | Linguistic Characteristics | Instructional Focus | Recommended Co-Teaching Model(s) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Beginning (Levels 1–2) | Limited understanding; single words/phrases; reliance on L1 connection.13 | Intensive scaffolding, sheltered vocabulary mastery, comprehension checks. | **Alternative Teaching** (L2 specialist provides intensive pre-teaching/boot camp); **Station Teaching** (focus on guided, concrete practice). |
| Intermediate (Levels 3–4) | Simple sentences, expanding academic vocabulary; need support for abstract ideas. | Bridging content and language; structured opportunities for L2 discourse and structured output. | **Parallel Teaching** (Reduces group size, maximizes safe participation, allows for varied scaffolding); **Station Teaching** (rotation of content application and language analysis tasks). |
| Advanced (Levels 5–6) | Near parity with peers; complex grammar; refinement of academic register in content areas. | Refinement of academic register; analytical reading; demonstration of complex, abstract understanding. | **Team Teaching** (For expert language modeling and immediate content clarification); **Content-focused Parallel Teaching** (e.g., for differentiated research projects). |

### **3.2. Model Selection Rationale by Proficiency Level**

* **Beginning Learners (WIDA Levels 1-2):** These students require frequent, explicit, high-input linguistic intervention, often relying on concrete, visual, and kinesthetic supports.14 Co-teaching models must facilitate separation into small groups to provide sheltered, high-input instruction.  
  **Alternative Teaching** is effective here, allowing the language specialist to pre-teach essential vocabulary or concepts immediately prior to the general lesson. **Station Teaching** is also highly useful for providing hands-on, concrete practice activities that reinforce early language structures and content vocabulary.  
* **Intermediate Learners (WIDA Levels 3-4):** The instructional focus for intermediate learners shifts to active L2 production, expanding academic vocabulary, and structured discourse. **Parallel Teaching** is exceptionally valuable as it reduces the group size, which, in turn, increases the wait time and frequency of student response—critical components for bridging developing language proficiency to academic function. Station Teaching remains useful for differentiating tasks, allowing one station to focus on content application and another on specific language analysis.  
* **Advanced Learners (WIDA Levels 5-6):** These students are highly proficient but require refinement of academic register, grammar complexity, and analytical thinking processes. They benefit most from the fully integrated, simultaneous modeling provided by **Team Teaching**. Team teaching allows both experts to seamlessly model complex academic language, engage in high-level content clarification, and challenge students to demonstrate complex, abstract understanding in L2.

## **IV. Adaptation for Bilingual and Dual Language Classrooms (L1 Integration)**

### **4.1. The WIDA Framework for Bilingual Collaboration**

For classrooms serving Spanish-English or Portuguese-English learners, co-teaching must integrate first language (L1) support to promote biliteracy and deep conceptual understanding. WIDA strongly advocates for collaboration that integrates language and content and builds on what students already know and can do.1 WIDA’s resources, such as the

*Marco ALE* for Spanish Language Arts, provide a system for aligning assessment and instruction in Spanish, which is vital for co-planning the dual language curriculum.15

### **4.2. Strategic Use of Translanguaging in Co-Taught Lessons**

The adaptation of co-teaching models for dual language settings centers on the strategic integration of **translanguaging**. Translanguaging is the intentional, fluid, and dynamic use of a student’s full linguistic repertoire (L1 and L2) to support access to complex texts and express academic thinking.4 This practice is fundamental to the principle of promoting

**equity between languages**, ensuring that English does not dominate instructional norms or material valuation.4

Successful adaptations include modifications to the high-intensity co-teaching models:

1. **Team Teaching Modification (Role Switching):** In a dual language context, co-teachers often switch roles throughout the day or unit. One teacher may deliver core content in English (L2) while the co-teacher provides targeted support using L1 translanguaging strategies, which might include comparing cognates, clarifying complex texts in the L1, or allowing students to draft initial ideas in their stronger language.4  
2. **Parallel Teaching Modification for Biliteracy:** This model is employed to manage biliteracy progression. Teachers co-plan weekly to align instruction, but use parallel structures to deliver content in separate languages. For instance, teachers use leveled nonfiction texts exploring the *same theme* (e.g., plant life cycles) tailored specifically to the progression goals in Spanish and English.4 This structured coordination supports cross-language transfer, ensures deep vocabulary development, and promotes thinking in two languages simultaneously.

A practical example from a WIDA-aligned dual language program demonstrates this integrated approach: co-teachers dedicate a 90-minute block weekly to co-plan, focusing on aligning reading strategies across both languages. During instruction, one teacher delivers English content while the other uses translanguaging strategies to provide targeted support.4

## **V. Operational Requirements and Program Sustainability**

### **5.1. The Critical Role of Co-Planning: Realistic Time Requirements and Data**

The sustainability of any co-teaching program rests heavily on the time allocated for collaborative planning. Failing to arrange for "at least periodic shared planning time" is consistently cited as a critical factor that causes co-teaching programs to fail.11 When there is no prior discussion between co-teachers, the assistance provided in the classroom often lacks connection to the curriculum, effectively reducing the co-teacher to an educational assistant without providing true instructional benefit to the student.10

Quantitative research establishes a minimum requirement: co-teachers must find at least **20 minutes minimum** once a week to meet.3 This time must be treated as "sacrosanct" and scheduled regularly.3 However, this 20-minute minimum is often sufficient only for brief logistical check-ins. It is grossly inadequate for the level of co-planning necessary for high-intensity models like Team Teaching or for the specific curriculum alignment required in dual language settings.

For districts aiming for high-fidelity implementation, particularly in bilingual contexts, the data indicates that successful programs utilize a dedicated **90-minute block weekly** for co-planning.4 The comparison between the 20-minute minimum and the 90-minute high-fidelity practice highlights a key policy implication: administrators must recognize the 20-minute mark as a logistical compliance threshold, not a measure of instructional excellence, and must budget resources to meet the higher requirement necessary for complex, standards-aligned collaboration.

### **5.2. Strategies for Finding and Protecting Collaborative Time**

Since time constraints are frequently cited as a failure mechanism, robust administrative strategies are necessary to protect and maximize shared planning time 11:

1. **Administrative Coverage:** Using other certified professionals—such as principals, counselors, department chairs, or specialists—to cover classes allows co-teachers to meet without interrupting instruction.11  
2. **Funding for Substitutes:** Utilizing grants, parent-teacher organization funds, or other sources to pay for substitute teachers to cover planning time.11  
3. **Structured Start Times:** Implementing independent work or partner learning activities at the beginning of a class period allows co-teachers to engage in brief, informal planning.11  
4. **Dividing Labor:** Co-teachers can save significant preparation time by dividing tasks, such as having one teacher prepare materials and copies for all lessons involved.11

### **5.3. Factors Driving Sustainability and Program Failure**

Co-teaching program sustainability is achieved when the required resources, fidelity, and professional responsibilities are consistently met.

Table 3: Requirements for Program Sustainability and Failure Avoidance

| Critical Sustainability Factor | Research Requirement/Benchmark | Impact/Risk of Failure |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Planning Time Commitment | **Minimum 20 minutes/week** of protected, collaborative time.3 | Failing to allocate time is the single greatest failure factor.11 |
| Instructional Fidelity | Consistent use of models that increase instructional intensity (Parallel, Station, Team).11 | Leads to students not receiving increased instructional intensity (Mistake \#6).11 |
| Dual Language Specifics | Dedicated, longer block (e.g., 90 minutes) for biliteracy alignment.4 | Prevents coordination of vocabulary, content, and cross-language transfer.4 |
| Accountability and Ownership | Shared responsibility for all student progress (joint accountability).11 | Lack of consensus diminishes the purpose and effectiveness of co-teaching.11 |
| Data Collection/Reflection | Must gather ongoing data on student achievement and utilize Co-reflecting.1 | Leads to stagnation and inability to prove program impact to stakeholders.11 |

It is insufficient for teachers simply to find the time; they must also "use that time wisely".11 This implies that programmatic failure often stems from a lack of effective co-planning strategy. Professional development must therefore instruct teachers on

*how* to use the WIDA proficiency descriptors, Language Expectations, and formative assessment data during their shared planning time to collaboratively identify language growth targets and design integrated, differentiated instruction.1

## **Conclusion: Moving Toward High-Fidelity, Equitable Co-Teaching**

The research establishes that co-teaching remains a valuable service delivery option for Multilingual Learners, provided it is implemented with high instructional fidelity and administrative support. The six Friend and Cook models are best utilized as flexible tools within the overarching WIDA Collaboration Cycle, not as static alternatives.

For co-teaching to effectively improve academic achievement and language development in bilingual students, the evidence mandates three primary policy shifts:

1. **Eliminate Low-Intensity Reliance:** Administrators must actively monitor co-taught classes to ensure teachers do not default to One Teach, One Assist, a model that violates instructional equity by limiting the EL’s access to the content expert and degrading the role of the language specialist.2  
2. **Mandate Strategic Model Selection:** Model choice must be directly driven by student language proficiency (WIDA levels). High-intensity, small-group models (Parallel and Station Teaching) should be strategically favored for Beginning and Intermediate learners, while Team Teaching is reserved for advanced learners needing sophisticated L2 modeling.  
3. **Resource Allocation for Planning:** Operational sustainability requires protected, recurring planning time. Districts supporting bilingual or dual language instruction must commit to scheduling a dedicated, lengthy block (e.g., 90 minutes weekly) necessary for the complex coordination of biliteracy goals and the seamless integration of L1 translanguaging strategies.4

Ultimately, co-teaching fails when it is treated as a structural compliance measure rather than an integrated, professional partnership focused on delivering specialized instruction and ensuring linguistic equity for all learners.
